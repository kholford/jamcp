Judith A McMahon — Care Plan
Strategy, Approach, and Rationale

1. Purpose

This document explains the overall care plan—what is being recommended, how it will be carried out, and why this approach is being taken.

The goal is to ensure clarity, alignment, and a plan that works both emotionally and practically.

The proposed plan balances:

  • Quality of care
  • Financial longevity
  • Practical execution
  • Emotional considerations

2. Care Needs & Transition

  • It’s getting harder to safely and consistently manage everything for care at home
  • Moving to assisted living would help by:
    • Providing adequate and appropriate daily care
    • Offering a safer environment
    • Reducing the overall burden on Chuck

3. Overall Strategy

This care plan is built upon a few core ideas:

  • Judy’s resources must be used to support her care first
  • The transition must be structured and manageable
  • Complexity should be reduced wherever possible
  • Decisions must support both short-term stability and long-term sustainability

4. Big Picture Timeline

At a high level, here is how the plan unfolds over time:

  1. Planning period
  2. Transition to assisted living
  3. Short transition period for home and relocation
  4. Sale of the home
  5. Assets fund care over approximately 8 years following home sale
  6. Medicaid (Title 19) funding begins after asset depletion

5. Planning Period

Approach

  • Structured planning window (approximately 4–8 weeks)
  • Focus:
  • Evaluate options and select assisted living facility
  • Begin organizing personal belongings
  • Prepare emotionally and logistically for transition

Rationale

  • Multiple major changes are happening at once
  • Moving too quickly creates stress and rushed decisions
  • Waiting too long increases risk

Balance:

  • Enough time to prepare so not rushed
  • Not open-ended; avoid increasing risk exposure

6. Transition to Assisted Living

At the conclusion of the planning period, the next step is the transition to assisted living.

  • Finalize facility selection, secure acceptance
  • Pack belongings, confirm logistics for move
  • Execute the move; care services begin

This represents:

  • A shift to a safer environment and greater support
  • The beginning of the financial execution of the plan

Monthly Cost Structure

  • Estimated Care Cost: $11,000 (conservative)
  • Less Social Security Income: ($3,193)
  • Net Monthly Asset Burn: $7,807

7. The House — Funding Foundation of the Plan

The house represents the primary financial foundation that enables the care plan.

Role in the Plan

  • Largest source of available funding
  • Enables multi-year care sustainability
  • Provides flexibility and financial stability
  • Supports approximately 8 years of private-pay care following sale
  • Provides a clear and controlled source of funding aligned with the care timeline

Selling the home within the defined transition window:

  • Converts illiquid equity into usable funds
  • Avoids forced early liquidation of investments and sequence-of-returns risk
  • Provides the framework within which Chuck’s transition occurs

Structural Importance

  • Not a discretionary decision point—sale is required for the plan to function as intended
  • Single largest factor that enables financial and structural integrity of the plan
  • Timing of home sale and Chuck’s transition are directly linked, and must be completed within the defined timeframe to keep the plan on track

8. Chuck’s Transition

Approach

  • Defined transition period (approximately two months following Judy’s move to assisted living)
  • Partial, short-term financial support provided, focused on housing-related expenses
  • Chuck participates financially by funding personal expenses and food during transition period
  • Relocation completed prior to, or concurrent with, the home sale

Rationale

This approach:

  • Provides time for Chuck to transition in a reasonable and respectful way
  • Provides structure and clarity for execution based on the plan
  • Avoids long-term financial or logistical complexity

Long-term occupancy is not practically viable because it:

  • Requires the use of funds needed for care
  • Adds ongoing complexity that must be managed
  • Is not financially sustainable without support
  • Does not improve long-term outcomes

9. Vehicle — Asset Simplification

Approach

  • Sell the vehicle early

Rationale

  • Not needed for ongoing use
  • Chuck has his own transportation
  • Simplifies financial structure
  • Eliminates unnecessary ongoing asset management
  • Increases liquid assets for care
  • Improves self-funding picture for facility qualification

10. Medicaid (Title 19) Integration

  • Assets are used to fund care during private-pay phase
  • No gifting or disqualifying transfers
  • Plan is designed to transition naturally into Medicaid after asset depletion
  • Projected depletion point: approximately Year 8.3 post-sale

Medicaid is not a fallback—it is the intended final phase of the funding strategy.

11. Chuck — Communication & Risk Management

Key Reality

This is the most sensitive part of the plan.

Chuck may feel:

  • Threatened by the change
  • Concerned about housing/displacement
  • Loss of role
  • No recognition for long-term presence

Why This Matters

These feelings may result in:

  • Emotional escalation
  • Withdrawal of care
  • Disruption of the plan
  • Acceleration of the transition

The biggest risk in this plan is behavioral, not financial.

Therefore, a structured, deliberate approach is needed to maintain stability and keep the plan moving forward.

Approach

Stabilize first

  • Have a confirmed plan for backup care that can be activated quickly if Chuck becomes unavailable or disengages
  • Avoid introducing disruption before that plan is in place

Prepare in advance

  • Identify and evaluate assisted living options
  • Have a preferred facility selected or narrowed down
  • Organize financial and legal elements (trust, accounts, plan)
  • Be operationally ready to transition quickly, if needed; otherwise execute plan accordingly to the established timeline

Communicate at the right time and with clarity

  • After key elements are prepared (care, facility, finances)
  • Frame the plan as thoughtful and necessary, not abrupt
  • Not: “This is what’s happening”
  • Instead: “Here’s what I’m seeing and why I think this is the right direction”
  • Keep the message clear and consistent without opening up unnecessary negotiation
  • State the plan as direction, not as a discussion with options
  • Explain the reasoning once, without inviting debate
  • Acknowledge impact on Chuck without changing direction
  • Avoid introducing alternatives under pressure
  • Use consistent language to reinforce the path forward (“the plan”, "the transition ", “the timing”)

Be ready to execute the plan more quickly if needed

12. Revocable Living Trust

This care plan strategy calls for the creation of a revocable living trust.

Purpose

The trust is to provide the structural framework that will enable the plan to be executed cleanly and as intended, to help ensure the desired outcomes are achieved in accordance with Judy’s wishes.

The primary objectives of the trust are to:

  1. Ensure Judy’s smooth transition into assisted living
  2. Facilitate orderly sale of residence
  3. Preserve assets for the self-pay phase of care funding
  4. Help ensure Medicaid qualification and avoid potential penalties (no gifting during lifetime)
  5. Provide clear, enforceable inheritance structure
  6. Address early-death timing scenarios with a consistent strategy
  7. Prevent disputes between partner and children
  8. Ensure funeral expenses are pre-funded and excluded from Medicaid spend-down

All of Judy’s key assets would be transferred into, and as such owned by, the trust, including but not limited to the following:

  • Residence (real estate)
  • Investment accounts (brokerage and traditional IRA)
  • Checking account
  • Vehicle
  • Funeral Trust and savings
  • Other personal property

Why It Matters

Without a trust:

  • Control is fragmented
  • The home may not be handled as intended
  • Execution becomes more difficult

With a trust:

  • Decisions and control of assets are centralized
  • The plan will be carried out as intended
  • Probate is avoided; inheritance rules are clear
  • Medicaid-compliant spend-down is executed
  • Account beneficiary designations are coordinated with overall plan

13. Co-Trustee — Role Responsibilities & Characteristics

The care plan is designed to be executed through a co-trustee structure. Both co-trustees have shared legal authority and either can execute on the defined responsibilities.

In practice, the co-trustee who is also the grantor of the trust, Judy in this case, would be the one to execute on those responsibilities “by default”. The other co-trustee could step-in to act in the event that Judy required assistance or became incapacitated, or in the event of her death.

Core Responsibilities

  • Oversee execution of the plan
  • Manage financial resources
  • Coordinate major actions (sale, payments, logistics)
  • Coordinate retirement account distributions as needed for care funding

Role Characteristics

  • Clearly defined
  • Time-bound
  • Structured to avoid ambiguity

14. Co-Trustee — Practical Execution & Impact

While the co-trustee role defines responsibility, execution requires consideration of real-world effort and coordination.

Execution Includes

  • Coordinating transition to assisted living
  • Managing home sale process
  • Handling financial and administrative tasks

Design Principle

The plan is intentionally structured to:

  • Avoid prolonged or open-ended responsibilities
  • Limit complexity
  • Provide a defined sequence of actions

This ensures execution remains manageable and sustainable.

15. Key Tradeoffs

This plan reflects a few key trade-offs that shape how it is structured and carried out.

Timeline for Planning & Transition Periods

  • Longer timeline:
  • Improves comfort and preparation
  • Increases exposure to health and execution risk

Conclusion: Maintain a structured, time-bound planning window and transition period

Home Retention Duration

  • Keeping the home longer:

    • Provides short-term emotional comfort
    • Increases financial cost and execution complexity

  • The timing of the home sale also directly enables Chuck’s transition, as both are tied to the same defined timeframe.

  • Extending home retention delays both financial execution and the ability to complete the transition process.

  • Current financial resources provide flexibility to optimize sale timing within a defined transition window

Conclusion: Timely sale supports both financial and structural integrity of the plan

16. Final Takeaway

This plan is designed to:

  • Ensure care needs are fully supported
  • Provide financial sustainability over approximately 8 years before Medicaid transition
  • Enable a structured and manageable transition
  • Maintain clarity, control, and consistency in execution

It replaces uncertainty with structure and allows decisions to be made deliberately rather than under pressure.

The approach balances:

  • Care quality
  • Financial responsibility
  • Practical execution
  • Consideration for all involved

The result is a plan that is not only sound in theory, but designed to work effectively in real-world conditions.
