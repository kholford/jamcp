Appendix D: Timing Flexibility & Transition Risk

1. Purpose

This appendix outlines the degree of flexibility available in the timing of transition to assisted living, and the tradeoffs associated with delaying that transition.

It is intended to support thoughtful decision-making—not to prescribe a specific timeline.

2. Bottom Line Summary

  • The timing of entry into assisted living is financially flexible within limits, provided assets are preserved
  • The primary constraints are not financial, but practical: care quality, safety, and transition risk
  • A delay of 1–3 months can be reasonable if managed intentionally, but risk increases with time
  • The key distinction is between a prepared delay and an unprepared delay

3. Core Financial Principle

If:

  • Monthly living expenses are covered by income (e.g., Social Security ~$3,300/month)
  • AND
  • No drawdown occurs from:
    • Brokerage (~$150,000)
    • Cash (~$30,000)

Then:

The starting asset base remains unchanged

And therefore:

  • The self-funding runway (approximately 5–6 years) remains unchanged
  • The difference between entering now vs. in 1–3 months does not materially impact long-term funding capacity

4. Why This Works

The financial model is driven by:

Starting Liquid Assets ÷ Monthly Care Shortfall = Duration of Self-Funding

If starting assets are preserved:

  • The duration of funding remains stable
  • Timing differences in the range of 1–3 months have minimal financial impact

5. Prepared vs. Unprepared Delay

This is the most important distinction.

Prepared Delay

A delay can be appropriate when:

  • Care needs are being adequately met
  • Backup care options are identified
  • Facility options are actively being evaluated
  • Legal and financial structures are in progress or complete
  • There is a defined timeline and next steps

This represents a controlled and intentional delay

Unprepared Delay

A delay becomes risky when:

  • Care is inconsistent or insufficient
  • No backup care is in place
  • Facility selection has not begun
  • Decisions are being deferred without structure
  • The timeline is undefined

This represents an uncontrolled delay with increasing risk

6. What Changes With Delay (Non-Financial Risks)

A. Care Risk (Primary Constraint)

  • Parkinson’s symptoms may progress
  • Increased risk of:
    • Falls
    • Medication issues
    • Unsafe conditions

This is the most important limiting factor

B. Facility Admission Risk

  • Health changes may:
    • Require a higher level of care
    • Limit available facility options
    • Increase cost

C. Transition Difficulty

  • Delayed transition may result in:
    • Greater physical difficulty
    • Increased emotional strain
    • More complex logistics

D. Home Sale Timing Risk

  • Delay may expose the home sale to:
    • Market variability
    • Seasonal slowdowns
    • Potential pricing impact

E. Informal Care Strain

  • Increased reliance on current caregiver (Chuck)
  • Risk of:
    • Burnout
    • Reduced consistency of care
    • Sudden disruption

7. Subtle Financial Risks

Even if major assets are preserved:

A. Expense Leakage

  • Food
  • Utilities
  • Medications
  • Maintenance

If these exceed income:

  • Assets may gradually decline

B. Property Condition Risk

  • Deferred maintenance may:
    • Reduce sale value
    • Increase time to sell

8. Medicaid (Title 19) Impact

  • No direct impact if:
    • No gifting occurs
    • Assets are preserved
    • No structural changes are made

A short, controlled delay does not negatively affect Medicaid eligibility

9. Practical Guidance

Reasonable Flexibility Window

  • 1–3 months → generally manageable if planned
  • Beyond that → risk increases more meaningfully

Decision Framework

Consider:

  1. Is current care adequate and reliable?
  2. Is risk increasing over time?
  3. Does delay make the eventual transition harder?
  4. Are we actively preparing during the delay?

10. Refined Summary Statement

“The timing of transition to assisted living is financially flexible within a limited window, provided assets are preserved. However, the true constraints are not financial, but practical—care adequacy, health risk, transition complexity, and execution readiness.”

Conclusion

A short delay can be appropriate if it is:

  • Intentional
  • Structured
  • Actively used for preparation

However:

The decision should ultimately be guided by care quality, safety, and readiness—not financial flexibility alone.

This appendix is intended to support a balanced decision that considers both emotional readiness and real-world risk.
